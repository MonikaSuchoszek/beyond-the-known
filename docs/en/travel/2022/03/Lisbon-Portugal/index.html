Lisbon with a Little One: Our First Family Trip Abroad

by Monika Suchoszek

20-03-2022
in Travel

My maternity leave was coming to the end

Following main shopping street until we reached The Rua Augusta Arch, a stone, memorial arch-like, historical building leading to open waterfront square. One of the biggest squares in Europe, Praca do Comercio with statue of King José I. In the neighbourhood there is also very famous Elevador de Santa Justa which feels like an unnecessary invention since street going to the top of the hill has couple hundred of meters and there are many more places where elevator would be much more useful. View point of Santa Justa lift was closed so we went immediately to check Archaeological museum that is located on the top of this hills with charming square in front of it.

The Carmo Ruins and Archaeological museum is definitely worth to check! It’s now the ruins of old Church of the Convent of Santa Maria do Carmo that was destroyed during the 1755 Lisbon Earthquake leaving it without roof. Reconstruction works started but were quickly abandon and impressive naves left without cover and the chapels incomplete. Skeletal remains of the huge church shows destruction caused by the earthquake. In 5 rooms, you can find there exhibitions with pieces from very different periods and cultures like Peruvian mummy or 4th century sarcophagus. Some walls are decorated with large and beautiful azuelejos tiles. The outside area is beautiful and great for interesting pictures. Entry is €5 per adult.

Lisbon has so many hills and steep stairs! However, this means that everyone will have a chance to find their favourite view point as there are plenty to choose! Jardim da Cerca da Graça with small park around was 5min walking distance from our apartment. It was a steep walk uphill but the area nearby was full of restaurants, small bakeries and shops with fresh fruits and vegetables.

Alfama is another interesting neighbourhood full of narrow streets, strething just below castle and few viewpoints: Miradouro de Santa Luzia and Miradouro das Portas do Sol. Get lost in the maze of narrow and beautiful streets, try cherry ginja and “salami” (kind of cookie) in one of the old bakeries.There are plenty of typical shops, cafes, bars, restaurants. In addition, Alfama is one of the places where fado shows can be experienced in Lisbon, we saw offers for the evening shows in front of the small typical restaurants. This part of the city first survived the great earthquake in 1755 and subsequent fires. The epicentre was in the middle of Atlantic Ocean and the biggest tsunami hit Lisbon shortly afterwards. Almost all buildings in the city were collapsed by tsunami, floated by the water or burned in fire. Alfama, was the only district that survived in a good condition with solid stone and high enough to avoid tsunami.

Our apartament was less than 15min walking from famous São Jorge Castle. There were many peacocks which live in the grounds of the castle, sitting on the trees and walking between the visitors, displaying their impressive tail plumage. Shaded gardens surrounding castle’s defensive walls, were great place to hide from strong midday sun. Stunning view of the central Lisbon and Tagus river there terrace along the ramparts. There was a small exhibition displaying items found within the castle. Very pretty streets around the castle. We were there quite early, around 10am and sizeable queue was already getting long but we used family line which was almost empty, it worked great everywhere in Lisbon. If you want to avoid waiting to buy tickets at the ticket office, buy it online in advance!

We left the Jerónimos Monastery and Belém Tower, two of the most visited sites in Lisbon, for the last day of our stay. First, you need to go and queue at the ticket office, and then, with your ticket, go straight to the entrance of the monastery. It’s not the same entrance, and it was confusing once we arrived there. There is also another line to enter the Church of Santa Maria de Belém, but this queue moves quite fast. The entrance to the church is free and it’s worth to stop by. You can see a few tombs there, including Vasco da Gama, the first European to reach India by sea. Afterwards, we finally headed to the monastery, and this time, again, we didn’t have to wait long because of family queue. We were a bit disappointed when, after maybe 30-40 minutes, we realized that there was nothing more to see. Besides the famous inner courtyard of the monastery, there were not many exhibits explaining history of this place. The main attraction is just walking the upstairs and downstairs corridors and admiring the architecture. Don’t forget to queue again for the famous pastéis from the legendary Pastéis de Belém down the road. We were not sure if these were our favorite pastéis. There is a small park close by where you can have a rest on the bench in the shade of the trees. We also took a walk to see the Belém Tower, but it was closed for renovation. Lunch in the nearby restaurant, Adega de Belém, was the perfect end to this day.

View from view point Miradouro de São Pedro de Alcântara, above Rossion station from where trains to Sintra leave.

Practical info:

  • The airport has perfect connection with the city by metro. We bought rechargeable card called Viva Viagem Card that we used for both of us for metro, tram and train. You pay for this card 0.5euro. Train to Cascais/Sintra/Estoril only 1.90euro per person, one way. Metro, one ride 1.35euro. Tram to Belem was around 1.5euro and if you buy ticket from the machine in the tram itself, it will be 3euro. Definitely very convenient to buy this card. It’s good to know more or less the prices for a single ride and where you want to go because you can’t get back the money left on the card. Whenever you scan the card, you can see how much is still on it. This is the way how we figure out how much was each ride and then we just charged the card again with more money. You can use this card also for the ferry (prices should be between 1-2euro)

  • We stayed in the city center and this was our biggest mistake because of the noise, until late evening hours. It happened few times that someone was discarding glass afer midnight, probably when restaurants were closing. We could hear every step, every conversation from the apartment above. Our idea was that if we stay in the city center, we could always come back to the apartment in the middle of the day so our baby can have a nap in the crip but ultimately she was napping in our soft-structured carrier (BabyBjorn, model Baby Carrier One)

  • Very friendly people, in the restaurants and shops. There were special lines for people with kids so we didn’t have to wait at the entrance to any tourist attraction.
  • It was almost impossible to find a restaurant that would have baby changing station in the toilet, it was actually very difficult to fit there with the baby at all. In the city center, space is limited so also it the restaurants, tables are close to each other.

Day trips: Sintra - Park of Pena - Moorish Castle - Villa Sassetti, Sintra from train station Rossio Carcavelos - walk on the beach - Santo Amaro train station Cascais - Boca do Inferno - Condes de Castro Guimarães Museum
